01 — The Business Problem

Why Netflix Obsesses Over Thumbnails

Netflix has over 300 million subscribers and a catalog of thousands of titles competing for attention at the same moment. When a user opens the app, they spend an average of 90 seconds browsing before they either pick something or leave. In that window, the single most powerful tool Netflix has is the thumbnail — the small rectangular image that represents each piece of content.

The question isn't aesthetic. It's economic. A thumbnail that drives more clicks drives more streams. More streams means more time spent on the platform. More time spent means subscribers are less likely to cancel. At Netflix's scale, even a 1–2 percentage point improvement in click-through rate across millions of impressions translates directly into retention and revenue.

But how do you know which thumbnail is actually better — not just which one you think looks better? You run an experiment.

02 — The Method

What A/B Testing Actually Is

An A/B test is a controlled experiment. You take a population of users, split them randomly into two groups, show each group a different version of something, and measure what they do. Because the split is random, the two groups are statistically equivalent on everything except the thing you changed. That means any difference in behavior you observe can be attributed to the change itself — not to who the users are.

This is the same logic that underlies clinical drug trials, policy experiments, and virtually every major product decision at technology companies. It's also a foundational application of machine learning thinking in business: you form a hypothesis, design a test, collect data, and let the evidence tell you what's true.

Why Random Assignment Is Non-Negotiable

Without randomness, the two groups will differ in ways you can't see or control. If you show the new thumbnail to returning subscribers and the old one to new users, any difference you observe could be driven entirely by subscriber loyalty — not the artwork. Random assignment neutralizes those hidden variables. It's what makes the experiment's conclusion trustworthy.

03 — The Experiment

What This Project Built

This project simulates a Netflix-style A/B test entirely in Python. One thousand users were randomly assigned to see one of two artwork versions — Artwork A or Artwork B — and their click behavior was simulated using known probabilities that mimic realistic engagement rates.

Total Users 1,000 Randomly assigned
Artwork A CTR 24% True click probability
Artwork B CTR 30% True click probability
Significance Level 5% Alpha threshold (α = 0.05)

Each simulated user was assigned a user ID, a group (A or B), and a click outcome (1 = clicked, 0 = did not click). The click outcome was generated probabilistically — not deterministically — meaning the observed rates in the data are close to but not exactly 24% and 30%, just as they would be in any real-world experiment.

04 — The Code

Complete Python Implementation

The full experiment — simulation, analysis, visualization, and statistical test — runs in a single Python script. Every step is annotated so the logic is transparent.

Python · netflix_ab_test.py 
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats
from statsmodels.stats.proportion import proportions_ztest

# ── STEP 1: SIMULATE THE EXPERIMENT ─────────────────────

# Lock in reproducibility — same seed = same results every run
np.random.seed(42)

n_users = 1000

# Randomly assign each user to Artwork A or B (50/50 split)
groups = np.random.choice(['A', 'B'], size=n_users)

# Simulate whether each user clicked based on their group's probability
clicks = []
for group in groups:
    prob = 0.24 if group == 'A' else 0.30
    click = np.random.binomial(1, prob)
    clicks.append(click)

# Build the DataFrame: one row per user
df = pd.DataFrame({
    'user_id' : range(1, n_users + 1),
    'group'   : groups,
    'clicked' : clicks
})

# ── STEP 2: CALCULATE SUMMARY STATISTICS ────────────────

summary = df.groupby('group').agg(
    total_users  = ('clicked', 'count'),
    total_clicks = ('clicked', 'sum'),
    ctr          = ('clicked', 'mean')
).reset_index()

print("=" * 40)
print("        A/B Experiment Summary")
print("=" * 40)
print(f"{'Group':<10} {'Users':<10} {'Clicks':<10} {'CTR':<10}")
print("-" * 40)
for _, row in summary.iterrows():
    print(f"{row['group']:<10} {int(row['total_users']):<10} {int(row['total_clicks']):<10} {row['ctr']:.2%}")
print("=" * 40)

# ── STEP 2B: BAR CHART ──────────────────────────────────

colors = ['#4C72B0', '#DD8452']
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 5))
bars = ax.bar(summary['group'], summary['ctr'], color=colors,
              width=0.4, edgecolor='white')

for bar, ctr in zip(bars, summary['ctr']):
    ax.text(bar.get_x() + bar.get_width() / 2,
           bar.get_height() + 0.005,
           f'{ctr:.2%}',
           ha='center', va='bottom',
           fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')

ax.set_title('Click-Through Rate by Artwork Version',
             fontsize=14, fontweight='bold', pad=15)
ax.set_xlabel('Artwork Version', fontsize=12)
ax.set_ylabel('Click-Through Rate (CTR)', fontsize=12)
ax.set_ylim(0, 0.45)
ax.yaxis.set_major_formatter(
    plt.FuncFormatter(lambda y, _: f'{y:.0%}'))
ax.spines['top'].set_visible(False)
ax.spines['right'].set_visible(False)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

# ── STEP 3: STATISTICAL TEST ─────────────────────────────

users_a        = summary.loc[summary['group'] == 'A', 'total_users'].values[0]
users_b        = summary.loc[summary['group'] == 'B', 'total_users'].values[0]
clicks_a_total = summary.loc[summary['group'] == 'A', 'total_clicks'].values[0]
clicks_b_total = summary.loc[summary['group'] == 'B', 'total_clicks'].values[0]

counts = np.array([clicks_a_total, clicks_b_total])
nobs   = np.array([users_a, users_b])

z_stat, p_value = proportions_ztest(counts, nobs)
alpha = 0.05

print("\n" + "=" * 40)
print("       Statistical Test Results")
print("=" * 40)
print(f"  Z-Statistic : {z_stat:.4f}")
print(f"  P-Value     : {p_value:.4f}")
print(f"  Significance: alpha = {alpha}")
print("-" * 40)
if p_value < alpha:
    print("  Result: STATISTICALLY SIGNIFICANT")
    print("  Artwork B outperforms Artwork A.")
else:
    print("  Result: NOT STATISTICALLY SIGNIFICANT")
print("=" * 40)
05 — The Results

What the Data Showed

After simulating 1,000 users and running both the summary analysis and the statistical test, the results were clear.

Click-Through Rate by Artwork Version

Artwork A CTR (observed) ~24% Baseline performance
Artwork B CTR (observed) ~30% Treatment performance
P-Value 0.013 Well below α = 0.05
Z-Statistic −2.49 Two-proportion z-test
Statistical Verdict

The difference between Artwork A and Artwork B is statistically significant (p = 0.013). There is only a 1.3% probability that a gap this large would appear by chance if the two artworks were truly performing identically. The experiment has enough evidence to conclude that Artwork B drives meaningfully higher engagement.

What the Statistical Test Actually Did

A two-proportion z-test was used to compare the click rates. This test is designed for exactly this situation: two independent groups, a binary outcome (clicked or didn't), and large enough samples for the math to be reliable. It answers one specific question — given these results, how likely is it that we're just seeing random noise? — and returns a p-value that quantifies that likelihood.

The threshold (alpha = 0.05) means we're willing to accept a 5% chance of calling a result significant when it isn't. With a p-value of 0.013, we're well inside that boundary.

06 — Business Interpretation

What It Means Beyond the Numbers

A click is not the end goal. It's a signal. When a user clicks a thumbnail, they're expressing enough interest to go further — to read a description, watch a trailer, or start streaming. At Netflix's scale, that signal is enormously valuable because it sits at the very top of the engagement funnel. Everything downstream — watch time, satisfaction ratings, renewal decisions — begins with a click.

A 6 percentage point improvement in CTR, sustained across tens of millions of daily impressions, would compound into a material difference in streaming hours and subscriber retention. It's the kind of gain that justifies an entire experimentation infrastructure.

What a Statistical Result Is Not

A p-value below 0.05 is an input to a decision, not the decision itself. Before acting on this result, a real product team would ask: Did users who clicked on Artwork B actually watch the content? Did they rate it well? Is Artwork B accurately representing the title, or is it generating curiosity clicks that lead to immediate drop-off? A deceptive thumbnail can inflate short-term CTR while quietly eroding user trust — and trust is the hardest thing to rebuild once it's lost.

07 — Business Recommendation

Ship Artwork B — With One Condition

The experiment returned a statistically significant result with a p-value of 0.013 — less than a 1-in-75 chance of being random noise. Artwork B outperformed Artwork A by approximately 6 percentage points in click-through rate. The evidence supports switching.

The condition: before a full rollout, confirm that the CTR lift translates downstream. Pull watch-time and satisfaction data for users who clicked Artwork B during the experiment. If engagement quality holds, ship it. If click rates went up but watch time or ratings dropped, Artwork B may be optimizing the wrong metric — and the right response is to iterate on the design, not blindly roll it out.

Experimentation doesn't end with a p-value. It ends when a decision has been made with confidence, accountability, and a clear understanding of what was measured and what wasn't.
